Welcome to Interactive Reaper Notes

This interactive guide provides quick access to notes and shortcuts for various features within the Reaper Digital Audio Workstation. Reaper is a powerful and highly customizable DAW, and these notes cover common functions to help streamline your workflow.

The notes are organized into categories: Workflow, Audio, MIDI, and Built-in Scoring. Use the navigation sidebar on the left (or above on smaller screens) to select a category and then a specific topic to view its details. Each section provides explanations, typical shortcuts, and tips for using Reaper effectively.

Musical Typing

This section describes how to use your computer keyboard as a MIDI input device in Reaper.

  • Action: Open Virtual MIDI Keyboard
  • Shortcut (Windows): Alt+B
  • Shortcut (Mac): Option+B
  • Notes: Allows you to use your computer keyboard to play MIDI notes. This is particularly useful when you don't have an external MIDI controller available.

Zooming

Efficient navigation often relies on quick zooming. Here are common ways to zoom in Reaper's arrange view and MIDI editor.

Vertical Zoom (Tracks):

  • Ctrl + Mousewheel (Windows)
  • Cmd + Mousewheel (Mac)
  • Up/Down Arrow keys (can be customized, may affect item selection depending on focus)

Horizontal Zoom (Timeline):

  • Mousewheel (default for horizontal zoom in arrange view)
  • Ctrl + Left/Right Arrow keys (Windows)
  • Cmd + Left/Right Arrow keys (Mac)

Other Zoom Actions:

  • Zoom to selection: Select items/time, then press Z (default action: "View: Zoom selected items")
  • Zoom out project (show all): Action "View: Zoom out project" (assign a shortcut or use Action List: Shift+?)
  • Zoom to time selection: Action "View: Zoom to time selection"

Track FX Chain

Reaper's FX chains are highly flexible. You can add, reorder, and manage audio effects on a per-track or per-item basis.

  • Open FX Chain: Click the "FX" button on the Track Control Panel (TCP) or Mixer Control Panel (MCP).
  • Add FX: In the FX Chain window, click "Add". Browse JSFX, VST, VST3, AU (Mac), etc.
  • Adjust Settings: Click the plugin in the chain to open its GUI.
  • Re-order FX: Drag-and-drop plugins within the FX Chain window.
  • Bypass FX: Click the power icon next to the plugin in the list, or Shift-click the plugin to bypass (Cmd-click on Mac).

Common Stock FX Modules (ReaPlugs & JSFX)

Reaper includes a comprehensive suite of built-in plugins (ReaPlugs) and JSFX text-based effects.

Compressor:

  • ReaComp: Highly versatile compressor. Good for individual tracks and bus compression. Offers detailed control over all standard compression parameters, plus sidechaining and various detection modes.
  • JS: LOSER/masterLimiter: Can be used as a compressor/limiter, often found useful on the master bus or for aggressive dynamic control.
  • JS: EventHorizonLimiter/Clipper: More of a limiter/clipper but can shape dynamics effectively, especially for preventing peaks.

EQ:

  • ReaEQ: Parametric EQ with unlimited bands, built-in spectral analyzer. Each band can be set to various filter types (low shelf, high shelf, band pass, notch, etc.).
  • JS: Equilibre: Graphic-style EQ, offering a different interface for equalization tasks.

Saturation and Analog Sounding Gear:

  • JS: Saturation: Simple saturation effect for adding harmonics and warmth.
  • JS: TapeSaturation: Simulates tape saturation characteristics, often adding a subtle compression and smoothing effect.
  • JS: Guitar/distortion/*: Various distortion and amp sim-like JSFX, useful for creative sound design or guitar processing.
  • Note: Many third-party plugins are popular for deeper analog emulation.

FX Chain Specifics

Diving deeper into some specific effect types available in Reaper's stock plugins.

Reverb:

  • ReaVerb: Convolution reverb. Load impulse responses (IRs) for realistic spaces. Can also generate early reflections and has a built-in EQ.
  • ReaVerbate: Algorithmic reverb, good for general ambiance and less CPU-intensive than ReaVerb for some applications.
  • JS: Gigaverb: Another algorithmic reverb option, providing a different character of reverberation.

Other Common JSFX:

  • Delay: ReaDelay, JS: Delay/delay, JS: Delay/ping_pong_delay. These offer various delay functionalities from simple echoes to complex rhythmic patterns.
  • Chorus: JS: Effects/chorus, ReaVocode (can be used for chorus-like effects). Add movement and width to sounds.
  • Transient Control: JS: Utility/transient_killer, ReaXcomp (multiband compressor, can be set to shape transients). Useful for emphasizing or softening the attack of percussive sounds.

Mastering (General Approach on Master Track)

While dedicated mastering engineers and tools are often preferred, Reaper's stock plugins can be used for basic mastering tasks on the master track.

  • Access Master Track: View -> Master Track (or Ctrl+Alt+M / Cmd+Option+M). The master track controls the final output of your project.

Typical Chain (using stock plugins):

  1. ReaEQ: For overall tonal balance, making subtle adjustments to the frequency spectrum.
  2. ReaComp: Gentle compression for "glue" (making the mix cohesive) or overall dynamic control.
  3. JS: Saturation/TapeSaturation (Optional): For subtle warmth or character.
  4. JS: LOSER/masterLimiter or JS: EventHorizonLimiter/Clipper: For final peak limiting to prevent clipping and achieve target loudness.

Note: Dedicated third-party mastering plugins are often preferred for critical mastering due to their specialized algorithms and features.

Advanced MIDI Expression

Reaper provides robust tools for fine-tuning MIDI performances and adding expressive details.

  • Open MIDI Editor: Double-click a MIDI item, or select it and press Ctrl+Alt+E (Windows) / Cmd+Option+E (Mac). This is where most MIDI editing takes place.

MIDI CC Lanes:

  • At the bottom of the MIDI Editor, click the "+" button or the lane type dropdown (e.g., "Velocity") to add/change lanes for CCs (Modulation, Pitch Bend, Expression, custom CCs).
  • Draw or edit CC data directly in these lanes to automate parameters of your virtual instruments.

Articulation Management (Key Switches / ReaControlMIDI):

  • Key Switches: Use notes (often outside the instrument's playable range) to trigger different articulations in your sampler/instrument plugin. Program these in the MIDI Editor's piano roll.
  • ReaControlMIDI: Add this JSFX plugin to a track to send specific MIDI messages (Program Change, Bank Select, custom CCs) for articulation changes, often useful for hardware synthesizers or complex sample libraries.
  • Third-party tools like Reaticulate offer more advanced articulation management.

MIDI FX Plugins (JSFX):

  • Add to track FX chain before the virtual instrument.
  • Examples: Arpeggiator (JS: MIDI/arp), Chord Generator (JS: MIDI/midi_chord), Humanizer (JS: MIDI/humanizer), Velocity Control (JS: MIDI/velocity_control). These can creatively alter or enhance MIDI performances.

Vocal Handling

Techniques for adjusting and correcting vocal performances in Reaper.

Gain Adjustment (Pre-FX):

  • Item Volume: Drag the top edge of an audio item up/down for quick, non-destructive gain changes.
  • Take Volume Envelope: Right-click item -> Take -> Take volume envelope. Allows for detailed volume automation within a single take.
  • Track Pre-FX Volume Envelope: Click "Trim" on track, select "Volume (Pre-FX)". Automates volume before any track effects are applied.

Pitch Correction:

  • ReaTune: Add as a Track FX. Use for automatic or manual pitch correction. In manual mode, you can draw pitch curves directly onto a representation of the audio.

Dynamics & EQ:

  • Use ReaComp and ReaEQ as needed to control dynamics and shape the tonal balance of vocals.

Vocal Alignment (Manual):

  • Visually align vocal takes by nudging items or using Stretch Markers.
  • Dynamic Split can help isolate phrases for easier alignment.
  • For automated alignment, ARA2 plugins like VocAlign or Revoice Pro are common (third-party).

Audio Timing, Volume and Pitch Correction

Reaper offers several tools for adjusting the timing, volume, and pitch of audio items.

Timing Adjustment (Stretch Markers & Dynamic Split):

  • Dynamic Split: Right-click item -> Item processing -> Dynamic split items... (splits item at transients or grid, useful for isolating individual notes or hits).
  • Stretch Markers:
    • View -> Stretch Markers (to ensure they are visible).
    • Add: Shift+Click on the waveform within an item.
    • Move: Drag stretch markers to warp audio timing non-destructively.
    • Rate Change: Alt+Drag (Windows) / Option+Drag (Mac) the edge of an item to time-stretch the whole item.
  • Quantize Audio Items: After splitting (e.g., with Dynamic Split), select items and use Action "Item: Quantize item positions to grid".
  • Finalize Edits (Glue): Select edited items, Right-click -> Glue items. This renders the changes into a new audio file.

Volume Correction (Item-based):

  • Item Volume: Drag top edge of item (as mentioned in Vocal Handling).
  • Item Normalize: Right-click item -> Item processing -> Normalize items (adjusts gain to a target peak or LUFS level).

Pitch Correction (Item-based or Track-based):

  • ReaTune (Track FX): As described under Vocal Handling.
  • Item Pitch Shift: Right-click item -> Item properties (or F2). Adjust "Pitch adjust (semitones.cents)".
  • Take Pitch Envelope: Right-click item -> Take -> Take pitch envelope. Allows for detailed pitch automation within a take.

Staff View (Notation Editor)

Reaper's MIDI Editor includes a notation view for working with musical scores. This allows for a more traditional approach to composing and editing MIDI data.

  1. Open a MIDI item in the MIDI Editor (Ctrl+Alt+E / Cmd+Option+E).
  2. In the MIDI Editor, go to View -> Notation editor (or click the musical notation icon in the MIDI editor's toolbar).
  3. Use toolbar tools (pencil, eraser, selection) to add, delete, or modify notes directly on the staff. You can also edit note durations, ties, and accidentals.
  4. Dynamics and other markings can often be entered using text events or specific CC messages, depending on your virtual instruments.
  5. Playback is linked to the main project playback, so you can hear your notation as you edit it.
  6. Export MIDI: File -> Export project MIDI. This allows you to take your MIDI data to dedicated scoring software like Finale or Sibelius for further layout and part extraction if needed.

Automation

Automating parameters (like volume, pan, or plugin settings) over time is crucial for dynamic mixes.

  1. Show Automation Lane: Click the "Trim" button on the Track Control Panel (TCP) or Mixer Control Panel (MCP).
  2. Select Parameter: From the dropdown menu that appears, choose the parameter to automate (e.g., Volume, Pan, Mute, or any FX plugin parameter by selecting FX -> [Plugin Name] -> [Parameter Name]).
  3. Create/Edit Points:
    • Click in the lane to add an automation point.
    • Drag points to change values.
    • Use Pencil tool (Alt+P / Option+P, or select from toolbar) to draw curves freely.
  4. Automation Modes (on track): Trim/Read (default - reads automation, allows trims), Read (reads automation only), Touch (writes automation while fader/knob is touched, returns to previous value on release), Write (writes automation continuously), Latch (writes automation while fader/knob is touched, stays at last value on release), Latch Preview.
  5. Multiple Lanes: A track can have many visible automation lanes for different parameters.
  6. Shape Segments: Ctrl+Drag (Windows) / Cmd+Drag (Mac) the segment between automation points to change the curve shape (e.g., linear, fast/slow curve, Bezier).

Offline Direct Processing (Apply FX / Render Take)

Applying effects or rendering items non-destructively or destructively.

Apply FX as New Take (Non-Destructive workflow):

  1. Select an audio item.
  2. Add FX to the item (Take FX) or its track (Track FX).
  3. Right-click the item -> "Apply track/take FX to items as new take". This renders the FX and creates a new audio take, preserving the original take underneath.

Render Item as New Take (More Options, can be destructive if overwriting):

  1. Select item(s).
  2. File -> Render... (or Ctrl+Alt+R / Cmd+Option+R).
  3. In Render dialog: Source: "Selected items", Bounds: "Time selection" or "Item length".
  4. Configure output format, then click "Render X file(s)". Choose "Add rendered items to new tracks in project" or import manually. If rendering to the same location and filename, it can be destructive.

Tempo and Meter Changes

Managing tempo and time signature changes within a project.

Tempo Map / Master Track Envelope:

  1. View -> Master Track (if not visible).
  2. Click the "Envelope" button on the Master Track's TCP.
  3. Check "Tempo map" to make it visible and editable.
  4. Click in the tempo map lane (usually at the top of the arrange view or below the timeline) to add tempo points. Drag points to change tempo. Right-click points for shape options (linear, square, slow start/end for gradual changes).

Insert Tempo/Time Signature Marker:

  • Place edit cursor where you want the change.
  • Insert -> "Time signature/tempo change marker..." (Shift+C opens Marker/Region manager, can also add from there).
  • Define new tempo and/or time signature in the dialog.

Gradual Tempo Changes:

  • Shape the tempo envelope curve between points to create accelerandos or ritardandos.

Surround Sound Support

Reaper supports multi-channel audio for surround sound mixing.

  1. Set Track Channels:
    • Click the "Route" button on a track's TCP or MCP.
    • In the routing window, set "Track channels" (e.g., 4 for Quad, 6 for 5.1, up to 64).
  2. Set Master Track Channels:
    • File -> Project Settings... (Alt+Enter / Option+Enter).
    • Go to the "Advanced" tab.
    • Set "Master track channels" accordingly.
  3. Surround Panning:
    • Add the ReaSurroundPan plugin (comes with Reaper) to tracks you want to pan in surround.
    • Configure ReaSurroundPan input/output channels to match your track/bus setup.
    • Use its interface to position sounds within the surround field.
  4. Export Surround Mix:
    • File -> Render...
    • In the Render dialog, ensure "Channels" matches your surround format (e.g., 6 for 5.1).
    • Choose appropriate output format (e.g., WAV, and consider interleaved vs. separate mono files for delivery).

Note: Ensure your audio interface and monitoring setup support surround playback for accurate mixing.

Imported Themes Setup

Reaper's appearance is highly customizable through themes. Users can download and install themes created by the community or make their own.

Finding Themes:

  • Reaper Stash: The official Reaper Stash website is the primary repository for themes, JSFX, scripts, and more.
  • Reaper Forums: The Cockos Reaper forums often have threads dedicated to theme releases and discussions.

Installing Themes:

  • Themes typically come as .ReaperThemeZip files.
  • Method 1 (Drag and Drop): Simply drag the .ReaperThemeZip file onto the Reaper main window. Reaper should prompt you to install and switch to the theme.
  • Method 2 (Options Menu):
    1. Go to Options -> Themes -> Theme Adjuster/Color Controls... (or simply Options -> Themes).
    2. In the Theme Adjuster window, you might find an "Import" or "Load theme" button, or you can navigate to your Reaper resource path (Options -> Show REAPER resource path in explorer/finder) and place the unzipped theme folder (if the .ReaperThemeZip contains a folder) into the "ColorThemes" subfolder. Then select it from the themes list.
  • After installing, the theme should appear in the Options -> Themes menu for selection.

Theme Adjuster:

  • Many themes support further customization via the "Theme Adjuster" (Options -> Themes -> Theme Adjuster/Color Controls...). This allows tweaking colors, layouts (if the theme supports WALTER scripting), and other visual elements without editing theme files directly.

ReaperConfigZip Files

Reaper allows users to export and import their entire configuration, including settings, themes, scripts, track templates, and more, as a single .ReaperConfigZip file. This is extremely useful for backing up your setup or migrating it to another computer.

Exporting Configuration:

  • Go to Options -> Preferences (Ctrl+P / Cmd+,).
  • In the Preferences window, navigate to the "General" page.
  • Click the "Export configuration..." button.
  • Select the components you wish to include in the export (e.g., themes, keymaps, mouse modifiers, project templates, track templates, FX chains, scripts, etc.).
  • Choose a location and save the .ReaperConfigZip file.

Importing Configuration:

  • Go to Options -> Preferences (Ctrl+P / Cmd+,).
  • In the Preferences window, navigate to the "General" page.
  • Click the "Import configuration..." button.
  • Select the .ReaperConfigZip file you want to import.
  • Choose which components from the zip file you wish to import. You can selectively import parts of a configuration.
  • Reaper will typically prompt to restart after importing a configuration.

Caution: Importing a configuration can overwrite your current settings. It's wise to export your current configuration as a backup before importing a new one.

Customization Capabilities

Reaper is renowned for its extensive customization capabilities, allowing users to tailor almost every aspect of the DAW to their specific workflow.

Key Areas of Customization:

  • Actions List (Shift+? or ?): The heart of Reaper's customization. Almost every command or function is an "action" that can be assigned to keyboard shortcuts, toolbar buttons, or MIDI/OSC controllers. You can also create custom actions (macros) by combining existing actions.
  • Mouse Modifiers (Options -> Preferences -> Editing Behavior -> Mouse Modifiers): Define different behaviors for mouse clicks and drags in various contexts (e.g., arrange view, MIDI editor, ruler) when combined with modifier keys (Shift, Ctrl, Alt, Cmd). This allows for incredibly fast and personalized editing workflows.
  • Toolbars: Create custom toolbars with any actions you frequently use. Toolbars can be docked or floating. Right-click a toolbar area -> Customize toolbar...
  • Menus (Options -> Customize menus/toolbars...): Modify existing menus or create entirely new ones.
  • Themes: As covered in "Imported Themes Setup," visual appearance is highly customizable. Advanced users can edit theme files directly (using WALTER for layout scripting).
  • Scripting (ReaScript): Reaper supports scripting in EEL2 (Reaper's native language), Lua, and Python. This allows for extending Reaper's functionality far beyond its built-in features. Scripts can be assigned to actions, toolbars, etc.
  • Track Templates & Project Templates: Save track setups (with FX, routing, etc.) as templates for quick recall. Project templates can define default project settings, tracks, and even content.
  • FX Chains: Save chains of plugins (with their settings) to quickly apply common processing.
  • Render Presets: Save common rendering settings (format, sample rate, etc.) for different export needs.

This deep level of customization means Reaper can be adapted to almost any audio task, from music production and mixing to post-production, sound design, and podcasting. It can take time to explore all the options, but the payoff is a DAW that works exactly the way you want it to.

Project Organization

Efficient project organization in Reaper helps streamline your workflow and keep complex projects manageable.

Project Templates:

  • Purpose: Save your entire project setup (tracks, routing, FX, sends, project settings, etc.) for quick recall when starting new projects.
  • How to Save: File -> Project templates -> Save project as template...
  • How to Load: File -> Project templates -> Load project template. Or, during new project creation, select a template from the "New project" dialog.
  • Benefit: Ideal for specific genres, mixing setups, or recording sessions that use the same basic track configurations.

Track Templates:

  • Purpose: Save individual tracks or groups of tracks with their FX, routing, sends, and automation item data.
  • How to Save: Right-click on a track's TCP or MCP -> Save tracks as track template...
  • How to Load:
    • Insert -> Track from template -> [Your Template Name]
    • Or drag-and-drop the `.RTrackTemplate` file from your Explorer/Finder into the project.
  • Benefit: Speed up adding pre-configured instruments, vocal chains, or drum buses.

Subprojects:

  • Purpose: Embed one Reaper project within another. The subproject appears as a single media item in the parent project.
  • Creation:
    • Select items on tracks -> File -> Save selected tracks/items as new project...
    • Or create an empty subproject: Insert -> New subproject.
  • Editing: Double-click the subproject item to open it in a new tab/window for editing. Save changes in the subproject to update the parent.
  • Benefit: Manage complex arrangements (e.g., mixing a full band track within a larger orchestral piece), work on sections independently, or collaborate by sharing subprojects. Improves performance by pre-rendering parts.

Advanced Take Management and Comping

Reaper's take system is robust for recording multiple passes and comping the best parts.

Fixed Media Item Lanes:

  • Purpose: Display takes as distinct lanes below the main track lane, allowing for easy visual comping.
  • How to Enable: Right-click on a track -> Takes -> Fixed item lanes.
  • Comping in Fixed Lanes:
    • Click on a section of a take in a lane to select it.
    • Use `T` (or next take action) to cycle through takes in the selected area.
    • Click and drag to define comp regions.
    • Right-click on the desired take section and choose "Crop to active take" or "Glue takes" to finalize the comp.
  • Benefit: Provides a clear, visual overview of all takes for easy selection and editing, similar to other DAWs' comping features.

Composite Take Sets:

  • Purpose: Group multiple takes together to be treated as a single composite take, allowing for more complex comping workflows.
  • How to Use:
    • Record multiple takes.
    • Right-click on the media item -> Takes -> Implode items to new takes.
    • Use take markers (Shift+T to insert) within takes to mark good sections.
    • Switch between different composite take sets to compare variations of your comp.
  • Benefit: Advanced organization for many takes, allowing A/B comparison of different comp versions.

MIDI Editor Views

The MIDI Editor in Reaper offers various views to suit different editing needs.

Piano Roll View:

  • Description: Default and most common view. Displays notes as horizontal bars on a piano keyboard grid.
  • Editing: Easily draw, resize, move, and copy notes. Velocity is typically shown as color intensity or in a dedicated lane below.

Drum Map View:

  • Description: Ideal for drums and percussion. Replaces the piano keyboard with named drum sounds (e.g., "Kick," "Snare," "Hi-Hat").
  • Setup:
    • Go to File -> MIDI drum map... in the MIDI Editor.
    • Map specific MIDI notes to custom names for clarity.
    • Save and load drum maps for different drum kits or projects.
  • Benefit: Much easier to visualize and edit drum patterns without memorizing MIDI note numbers.

Event List View:

  • Description: Shows MIDI events (notes, CCs, pitch bend, etc.) as a list with their timestamp, type, and value.
  • Use Case: Precise editing of individual event values, especially useful for troubleshooting or fine-tuning controller data.

Notation (Staff) View:

  • Description: Displays MIDI notes as traditional musical notation on a staff.
  • Use Case: Composing or editing MIDI using standard musical language, useful for musicians familiar with sheet music.

Controller Lanes (CC Lanes):

  • Description: Customizable lanes at the bottom of the MIDI Editor for drawing and editing MIDI CC (Continuous Controller) data like velocity, modulation, pitch bend, sustain, expression, or custom CC messages.
  • Adding/Removing Lanes: Click the "+" button or the lane type dropdown to add/remove desired CC lanes.
  • Editing: Draw automation curves using the pencil tool or manipulate points.

MIDI Editing Techniques

Enhance and refine your MIDI performances with these tools.

Step Recording:

  • Purpose: Record MIDI notes one step (e.g., one beat or division) at a time, rather than in real-time.
  • How to Use:
    • Set the grid appropriately in the MIDI Editor.
    • Go to Options -> "Enable step input" in the MIDI Editor.
    • Play notes on your MIDI keyboard or computer keyboard. Each note played will be inserted at the current edit cursor position for the defined duration, and the cursor will advance.
  • Benefit: Ideal for complex rhythms or precise note entry without timing pressure.

Quantizing MIDI:

  • Purpose: Align MIDI notes to a grid (e.g., quarter notes, eighth notes) to correct timing inaccuracies.
  • How to Quantize:
    • Select the MIDI notes you want to quantize in the MIDI Editor.
    • Go to Edit -> Quantize... (or use Action List: "MIDI editor: Quantize notes").
    • Set quantize strength, grid division, and other parameters in the dialog.
  • Quantize Strength: Allows partial quantization to retain some human feel.
  • Grids: Use appropriate grid settings for your musical context (e.g., 1/16 notes for tight drums, 1/8 notes for basslines).

Humanizing MIDI:

  • Purpose: Add subtle random timing and velocity variations to quantized or stiff MIDI performances to make them sound more natural.
  • How to Humanize:
    • Select the MIDI notes you want to humanize.
    • Go to Edit -> Humanize... (or use Action List: "MIDI editor: Humanize notes").
    • Adjust parameters like timing variance, velocity variance, and pitch variance.
  • JS: MIDI/humanizer: Can be added as a MIDI FX to a track for real-time humanization.

Arpeggiator / Chord Generators:

  • Purpose: Generate arpeggios (broken chords) or full chords from single notes.
  • JSFX: Use the JS: MIDI/arp plugin for arpeggios and JS: MIDI/midi_chord for chord generation. Place these in the FX chain *before* your instrument plugin.
  • Benefit: Create complex melodic patterns or full harmonic structures from simple MIDI input.

Notation Editor Features (Built-in Scoring)

Reaper's MIDI Editor includes comprehensive notation features for score creation and editing.

Score Creation & Editing:

  • Access: Open a MIDI item in the MIDI Editor, then select View -> Notation editor or click the notation icon in the toolbar.
  • Note Entry:
    • Use the pencil tool to click notes directly onto the staff.
    • Adjust note duration, ties, tuplets, and accidentals from the toolbar or context menus.
    • Can also convert existing piano roll MIDI data to notation.
  • Clefs & Key Signatures: Change clefs (e.g., treble, bass) and key signatures as needed for the staff.
  • Time Signatures: Automatically reflect project time signature, or manually adjust if needed.
  • Dynamics & Articulations: While not a full-fledged scoring program, basic dynamics and articulations can be represented, often by mapping them to MIDI CCs or using text events.
  • Parts and Staves: For tracks with multiple MIDI channels or instruments, you can configure multiple staves to display separate parts.

Integration with MIDI & Audio:

  • Synchronized Playback: Edits made in the notation view instantly update the underlying MIDI data and vice-versa. Playback is always synchronized with the main project.
  • Audio Rendering: The notation translates directly to MIDI data, which then triggers your virtual instruments for audio output.

Exporting Scores:

  • MIDI Export: File -> Export project MIDI. This exports the MIDI data from your project, which can then be imported into dedicated scoring software (like Finale, Sibelius, Musescore) for professional-grade layout, printing, and part extraction.
  • PDF/Image Export (via other software): While Reaper does not directly export to PDF or image formats for scores, exporting MIDI and then importing into a dedicated scoring program is the standard workflow for this.

Benefit: Allows musicians who prefer working with traditional notation to compose and edit directly within their DAW, bridging the gap between sequencing and scoring without needing to switch applications until final print/layout is required.

Audio to MIDI Conversion

Reaper provides several ways to convert audio transients or pitch information into MIDI notes.

Dynamic Split for Transient Detection (for drums/percussion):

  • Purpose: Analyze an audio item (e.g., a drum loop) to detect transients and create MIDI notes at each detected transient.
  • How to Use:
    • Select the audio item.
    • Right-click the item -> Item processing -> Dynamic split items...
    • In the Dynamic Split dialog, choose "Split selected items, create new MIDI item" or "Create new MIDI item with notes at transient positions".
    • Adjust sensitivity and threshold parameters to accurately detect transients.
    • A new MIDI item will be created on a new track or existing track, with notes placed at the detected transients.
  • Benefit: Useful for drum triggering, replacing drum sounds, or quantizing audio-based rhythms.

ReaTune for Pitch-to-MIDI (Monophonic Audio):

  • Purpose: Convert monophonic audio (e.g., a vocal line, bassline, or single instrument melody) into MIDI notes.
  • How to Use:
    • Add ReaTune to the audio track as an FX.
    • In ReaTune, enable "Send MIDI to track" or "Send MIDI to track/sub-tracks" (if you have a separate MIDI track for recording).
    • Set the track to record MIDI output.
    • Play the audio, and ReaTune will analyze the pitch and send corresponding MIDI notes.
    • Record the MIDI output onto a new MIDI item.
  • Limitations: Best for monophonic audio. Polyphonic conversion is generally not reliable with ReaTune.

ARA2 Plugin Integration (e.g., Melodyne, Revoice Pro):

  • Purpose: For advanced and accurate polyphonic audio to MIDI conversion, and detailed pitch/timing correction.
  • How to Use:
    • Install an ARA2-compatible plugin (e.g., Celemony Melodyne, Synchro Arts Revoice Pro).
    • Insert the ARA2 plugin as a track FX (it will typically appear in the FX browser with an ARA tag).
    • The audio on the track will be "sent" to the plugin for analysis.
    • Within the plugin's interface, you can typically export the recognized notes as MIDI.
  • Benefit: Industry standard for highly accurate pitch detection and polyphonic audio manipulation, often used for vocals and harmonic instruments.

Deep Customization

Reaper's core strength lies in its unparalleled customization. Go beyond the default to truly make Reaper your own.

Actions List (Shift+? or ?):

  • Access: Press Shift+? or `?` (or Actions -> Show action list).
  • Search: Type keywords to find specific functions (e.g., "quantize", "midi editor", "zoom").
  • Assign Shortcuts: Select an action, click "Add..." to assign a keyboard shortcut or MIDI/OSC trigger.
  • Find Shortcut: Click "Find shortcut..." and press a key combination to see which action it's assigned to.

Custom Actions (Macros):

  • Purpose: Combine multiple existing actions into a single new action.
  • How to Create:
    • In the Actions List, click "New action" -> "New custom action".
    • Drag actions from the left pane to the right pane to build your sequence.
    • Name your custom action and save it. It will now appear in the main Actions List and can be assigned a shortcut.
  • Benefit: Automate repetitive tasks, create complex workflows with a single keypress.
  • Example: "Normalize item, then add ReaComp."

Mouse Modifiers:

  • Access: Options -> Preferences -> Editing Behavior -> Mouse Modifiers.
  • Contexts: Customize mouse behavior for various areas (e.g., "Media item," "MIDI piano roll note," "Ruler").
  • Modifier Keys: Define actions for Left Click, Ctrl+Left Click, Shift+Left Click, Alt+Left Click, etc.
  • Benefit: Drastically speed up editing by tailoring mouse interactions to your exact needs.
  • Example: Set `Ctrl+Alt+Left Drag` on a media item to directly adjust item pitch.

Custom Menus and Toolbars:

  • Access: Options -> Customize menus/toolbars...
  • Edit Existing: Add, remove, or reorder items in Reaper's default menus (e.g., File, Edit, View).
  • Create New Toolbars: Design custom toolbars with frequently used actions or custom actions.
  • Benefit: Place your most important functions exactly where you need them for faster access.

ReaScript (Scripting):

  • Languages: Reaper supports Lua, Python, and EEL2 (Reaper's native language).
  • Accessing Scripts: Scripts can be found in the Actions List (they appear as actions).
  • Writing Scripts: Actions -> Show action list -> "New action" -> "New ReaScript".
  • Benefit: Extend Reaper's functionality by writing custom code to perform tasks not otherwise possible, or to automate highly specific, multi-step operations.
  • Community Resources: Many useful scripts are available on the Reaper Stash and forums.

MIDI Routing and MIDI Buses

Reaper's flexible routing system extends to MIDI, allowing complex instrument setups and MIDI effects chains.

Basic MIDI Routing (Hardware/Software Instruments):

  • Input MIDI to Track: Right-click on a track's record arm button -> Input MIDI -> [Your MIDI Device] -> [All Channels/Specific Channel].
  • Route MIDI to Instrument: Add your virtual instrument plugin (VSTi/AUi) to the track's FX chain. The MIDI data on the track will automatically be sent to the first instrument plugin in the chain.
  • Route MIDI to Hardware: Add the MIDI: MIDI over USB JSFX plugin to a track's FX chain. Configure its output to your desired MIDI hardware device.

MIDI Buses / Sends:

  • Purpose: Send MIDI data from one track to another, or to multiple tracks simultaneously.
  • How to Create a MIDI Send:
    • Click the "Route" button on the source track (the track sending MIDI).
    • In the Routing window, click "Add new send..." and select the destination track.
    • In the send matrix, ensure the "MIDI" checkbox is enabled. You can specify which MIDI input channel from the source is sent to which MIDI output channel on the destination.
  • Benefit:
    • Layering Sounds: Send one MIDI performance to trigger multiple virtual instruments.
    • MIDI FX Chains: Send MIDI through a dedicated MIDI FX track (e.g., an arpeggiator or chord generator) before it reaches the instrument track.
    • Control Multiple Instruments: Control several instruments from a single MIDI input.

Dedicated MIDI FX Tracks:

  • Concept: Create a track solely for MIDI processing (e.g., a track with a `JS: MIDI/arp` plugin).
  • Workflow: Send MIDI from your input track to this MIDI FX track, then send the output of the MIDI FX track to your instrument track(s).
  • Benefit: Centralize MIDI processing, apply the same MIDI effects to multiple instruments, or use multiple MIDI effects in series.

ReaFir (FFT EQ/Dynamics)

ReaFir is a powerful FFT (Fast Fourier Transform) based equalizer and dynamics processor included with Reaper. It excels at surgical EQ, noise reduction, and advanced filtering.

Key Modes:

  • EQ (Precise Equalization):
    • Purpose: Draw precise EQ curves on a spectral display.
    • How to Use: Select "EQ" mode. Draw points on the frequency spectrum to boost or cut. Ideal for surgical cuts (e.g., removing resonant frequencies) or gentle broad boosts.
    • Benefit: Visually intuitive and highly precise frequency manipulation.
  • Dynamics (Multiband Compressor/Expander):
    • Purpose: Apply multiband compression or expansion based on frequency.
    • How to Use: Select "Dynamics" mode. Define frequency bands and set compression/expansion parameters for each.
    • Benefit: Fine-tune dynamics across different frequency ranges, useful for de-essing, taming harsh highs, or bringing out low-end punch.
  • Subtract (Noise Reduction/De-click):
    • Purpose: Analyze a noise profile and subtract it from the audio.
    • How to Use:
      1. Insert ReaFir on the track.
      2. Select "Subtract" mode.
      3. Enable "Automatically build noise profile (B)" and play a section of the audio that contains *only* the noise you want to remove (e.g., silence with hiss, hum, or clicks).
      4. Disable "Automatically build noise profile (B)" once the profile is built.
      5. ReaFir will now attempt to remove that noise from the entire audio. Adjust reduction amount as needed.
    • Benefit: Effective for cleaning up recordings with consistent background noise.
  • Gate (Frequency-dependent Gate):
    • Purpose: Gate frequencies below a certain threshold.
    • How to Use: Select "Gate" mode. Set a threshold on the spectral display. Frequencies below this threshold will be attenuated.
    • Benefit: Useful for removing unwanted noise in specific frequency ranges that falls below a certain level.

General Tips for ReaFir:

  • FFT Size: Larger FFT sizes give more frequency resolution but higher latency. Smaller sizes are better for transient material.
  • Freeze Graph: You can "freeze" the spectral display to analyze a specific moment in time.
  • Preview Filtered: Option to listen only to the noise being removed in "Subtract" mode to ensure you're not cutting out desired audio.

ReaXComp (Multiband Compressor)

ReaXComp is Reaper's powerful multiband compressor, allowing independent dynamic control over different frequency ranges.

Core Functionality:

  • Multiband Processing: Divide the audio spectrum into up to 6 user-defined bands.
  • Independent Compression: Each band has its own full set of compressor parameters (threshold, ratio, attack, release, gain).
  • Visual Feedback: Provides a real-time graph of input and output levels for each band.

How to Use ReaXComp:

  1. Insert Plugin: Add ReaXComp to a track's FX chain.
  2. Define Bands: Click and drag the crossover points on the frequency spectrum display to define your desired frequency bands. You can add more bands by clicking on the crossover lines.
  3. Configure Each Band:
    • Select a band by clicking its associated control block.
    • Adjust the compressor parameters for that band:
      • **Threshold:** The level at which compression begins.
      • **Ratio:** How much the signal is compressed above the threshold.
      • **Attack:** How quickly compression engages.
      • **Release:** How quickly compression disengages.
      • **Gain:** Makeup gain for the band after compression.
  4. Link Bands (Optional): Option to link parameters between bands for more unified control.
  5. Output Gain: Adjust the overall output gain of the plugin.

Common Applications:

  • Mastering: Control dynamics across the entire mix (e.g., subtly compress lows, tame harsh mids, smooth highs).
  • Vocal De-essing: Isolate and compress harsh "s" sounds in the high-mid frequency range.
  • Bass Guitar: Compress low-end independently from mid-range attack to achieve a consistent, punchy sound.
  • Drums: Shape transients and sustain for individual drum elements or drum buses.
  • Sound Design: Creative dynamic shaping.

ReaLimit (Brickwall Limiter)

ReaLimit is Reaper's dedicated brickwall limiter, essential for maximizing loudness and preventing clipping at the final stage of mixing or mastering.

Core Functionality:

  • Prevent Clipping: Ensures the audio signal never exceeds a specified output ceiling.
  • Maximize Loudness: Allows you to increase the overall perceived loudness of your mix without introducing digital clipping.
  • Inter-Sample Peak Detection: Offers a mode to catch peaks that occur *between* samples, which can cause distortion even if sample peaks are below 0dBFS.

How to Use ReaLimit:

  1. Insert Plugin: Place ReaLimit as the *last* plugin on your master track FX chain.
  2. Set Ceiling: Set the "Limit" parameter to your desired maximum output level (e.g., -0.3 dBFS or -1.0 dBFS to avoid inter-sample peaks). This is your brickwall.
  3. Adjust Gain/Threshold: Increase the "Gain" (or reduce the "Threshold," depending on the interface design, though ReaLimit uses "Gain") to push the signal into the limiter and achieve desired loudness. The amount of gain reduction will be shown.
  4. Enable True Peak Limiting (Optional but Recommended): Check "True Peak Limiting" to prevent inter-sample peaks, which can cause distortion on playback systems, especially in lossy formats like MP3.
  5. Lookahead: Adjust "Lookahead" to allow the limiter to analyze the incoming signal slightly before it reaches the threshold, minimizing distortion. Longer lookaheads can introduce more latency.

Important Considerations:

  • Placement: ALWAYS last on the master track. Any FX after a limiter can cause clipping.
  • Avoid Over-Limiting: Too much limiting can crush dynamics and lead to a lifeless, distorted sound. Aim for transparent loudness.
  • Monitoring: Listen carefully for pumping, distortion, or loss of punch.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Practical steps to diagnose and resolve common problems in Reaper.

Audio Glitches and Dropouts:

  • Increase Buffer Size:
    • Where: Options -> Preferences -> Audio -> Device.
    • Action: Increase the "Audio buffering" (or "Block size" / "Buffer size") setting. A larger buffer reduces CPU strain but increases latency.
    • When: During mixing with many plugins or virtual instruments. Decrease for recording to reduce latency.
  • Adjust FX Processing Settings:
    • Where: Right-click on a track's FX button -> "FX processing settings".
    • Action: Set "Anticipative FX processing" to "No anticipative FX processing" for tracks with real-time input (e.g., live monitoring). Re-enable for mixing.
    • Benefit: Prevents crackles during live input monitoring by reducing lookahead processing.
  • Disable Unused Plugins/Tracks: Temporarily bypass or disable FX on tracks not currently being worked on. Mute unused tracks.
  • Render/Freeze Tracks: Render instrument tracks to audio (right-click track -> "Render/freeze tracks") to free up CPU from demanding VSTi's.
  • Check Sample Rate/Bit Depth Mismatch: Ensure your project settings (File -> Project Settings) match your audio device settings.

Interface Issues / Missing Windows:

  • Restore Default Screen Set:
    • Action: View -> Screen sets -> Default screen set.
    • Benefit: Resets all docked windows, toolbars, and layouts to a default state, often resolving issues with disappearing or misplaced elements.
  • Toggle Window Visibility: Use the "View" menu to explicitly show or hide specific windows (e.g., Mixer, Media Explorer, MIDI Editor).
  • Reset Preferences (Last Resort):
    • Action: Options -> Show REAPER resource path in explorer/finder. Close Reaper. Delete `reaper.ini` (and potentially `reaper-kb.ini` for keybinds) in the resource path.
    • Caution: This will reset ALL your Reaper preferences to default. Back up your `reaper.ini` first!

Recording Issues:

  • Input Monitoring:
    • Action: Ensure the track's monitor button (speaker icon) is enabled for hearing your input.
    • Modes: Right-click monitor button for different modes (e.g., "Monitor input (tape auto style)", "Monitor input (external MIDI/FX output)").
  • Record Modes:
    • Where: Right-click the track's record arm button -> "Record mode".
    • Options: "Record: input (audio or MIDI)", "Record: MIDI overdub", "Record: MIDI replace". Choose the appropriate mode for your recording scenario.
    • Benefit: Ensures you're recording how you intend to (e.g., creating new takes, layering MIDI, or overwriting).
  • Arm Track: Ensure the track's record arm button is red (enabled) for recording.
  • Input Selected: Verify the correct audio or MIDI input is selected for the track (right-click record arm button).

Web Browser Interface (Reaper Web Interface)

Reaper can host a web server that allows control of project parameters from any web browser on the same network, including mobile devices.

Setting Up the Web Interface:

  1. Enable Web Server:
    • Go to Options -> Preferences -> Control/OSC/web.
    • Click "Add" -> "Web browser interface".
    • Configure the port number (default is 8080) and optionally set a username/password for security.
    • Ensure "Enable web interface" is checked.
  2. Access from Browser:
    • On another device (phone, tablet, or another computer) connected to the same local network, open a web browser.
    • Enter the IP address of the computer running Reaper, followed by the port number (e.g., `http://192.168.1.100:8080`).
    • You might need to adjust firewall settings on the Reaper host computer to allow incoming connections on that port.

Functionality:

  • Transport Control: Play, Stop, Record, Loop.
  • Track Controls: Adjust track volume, pan, mute, solo, arm for recording.
  • Custom Layouts: You can create custom web interfaces using HTML/CSS/JavaScript for very specific control needs.
  • Metronome: Toggle metronome on/off.

Use Cases:

  • Remote Control for Performers: Musicians can control their own monitor mix or enable/disable recording from within the booth.
  • Live Performance: Trigger cues, change scenes, or adjust levels from a tablet on stage.
  • Home Studio Convenience: Control Reaper from your couch or other rooms.

Project Media/FX Bay

The Project Media/FX Bay is a central hub for managing all media items and FX used in your current project, making it easier to organize, locate, and manipulate project resources.

Opening the Bay:

  • Action: View -> Project Media/FX Bay (or type "Project Media/FX Bay" in the Actions List).
  • Location: It can be docked as a tab or float as a separate window.

Key Sections and Functionality:

  • Media Items:
    • List: Displays all audio, MIDI, and video items currently in your project.
    • Locate: Double-click an item in the list to select it in the arrange view.
    • Manage Files: Right-click an item to view its source file, open in editor, or replace the source file.
    • Clean Up: Identify unused media files in your project, allowing you to remove them to reduce project size or keep things tidy.
  • FX:
    • List: Shows all active plugins (VST, JSFX, AU, etc.) used across all tracks and items in the project.
    • Open GUI: Double-click an FX in the list to open its graphical user interface.
    • Locate FX: See which tracks or items an FX is applied to.
    • Offline FX: Quickly offline/bypass plugins to save CPU, especially useful for demanding FX.
    • Batch Processing: In some cases, you can select multiple instances of the same plugin and adjust parameters simultaneously (e.g., if you have the same EQ on multiple tracks).
  • Source Media (Optional):
    • List: Shows all the actual audio/MIDI files linked to items in your project, not just the items themselves.
    • Management: Useful for managing media assets, checking file paths, or consolidating project media.

Benefit:

  • Provides a bird's-eye view of your project's components.
  • Facilitates quick navigation, editing, and optimization of media and effects.
  • Indispensable for larger, more complex projects where keeping track of many items and plugins can be challenging.

ReaRoute (Audio Routing Driver)

ReaRoute is a virtual audio driver that comes with Reaper, enabling you to route audio between Reaper and other applications on your computer.

Purpose and Functionality:

  • Virtual Audio Cables: Acts as a set of virtual audio cables within your operating system.
  • Inter-Application Audio: Allows you to send audio from:
    • Reaper to another application (e.g., streaming software, a standalone instrument, or another DAW).
    • Another application into Reaper for recording or processing.
  • Multi-Channel: Supports multiple channels, allowing for stereo or even multi-channel audio routing.

Setting Up ReaRoute:

  1. Enable in Reaper:
    • Go to Options -> Preferences -> Audio -> Device.
    • Under "Audio System", select ASIO (Windows).
    • In the ASIO configuration, ensure ReaRoute is enabled as a device. You may need to click "ASIO Configuration..." to see its options.
  2. Send Audio from Reaper:
    • On a Reaper track's "Route" window, add a new hardware output send.
    • Select "ReaRoute" and choose the specific ReaRoute channel(s) you want to send to.
  3. Receive Audio in Another Application:
    • In the other application (e.g., OBS, Audacity, a standalone VST host), go to its audio preferences/input settings.
    • Select "ReaRoute" as an input device and choose the corresponding channels you sent from Reaper.
  4. Receive Audio in Reaper:
    • On a Reaper track, arm it for recording.
    • Right-click the record arm button -> Input: mono/stereo -> "ReaRoute" and select the channels you want to receive from another application.

Common Use Cases:

  • Live Streaming: Send Reaper's output (or submixes) directly to streaming software like OBS Studio.
  • Inter-DAW Routing: Send audio from one DAW to Reaper for specific processing (e.g., using Reaper's advanced routing or custom actions) or vice-versa.
  • External Signal Processing: Route audio to standalone effects processors or virtual instruments.
  • Recording System Audio: Capture audio playing on your computer (e.g., from a web browser, media player) directly into Reaper.
